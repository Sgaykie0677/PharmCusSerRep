Section 1: Systems Overview

This module introduces the core systems used by Pharmacy Customer Service Representatives (PCSRs). You will learn how to access, navigate, and utilize each platform effectively.

1.1 OKTA Access & Login

OKTA is the secure single sign-on (SSO) gateway for all Rightway systems.

Key Points

  • All systems (Tredium, MHK, Advocate, Client Matrix) are accessed through OKTA.
  • Never share login credentials.
  • Log out when stepping away from your workstation.

Login Workflow

  1. Open the OKTA dashboard.
  2. Select the system tile (Tredium, MHK, Advocate, etc.).
  3. System launches automatically via SSO.

1.2 Tredium Navigation & Functions

Tredium is the primary system for claims processing, member research, and benefit verification.

Main Navigation Icons

  • Menu (≡): Opens or collapses the left navigation panel.
  • House icon: Returns to the first opened blade.
  • Eye with slash: Closes all blades except the one selected.
  • Large X: Closes all blades (use cautiously).

Key Blades

Patient Blade

  • Cardholder Number & Person Code: Identifies member and dependents.
  • Address Tab: Physical, mailing, school, and alternate addresses.
  • Benefit Balances: Deductible and out-of-pocket accumulators.
  • Communication: Phone, email, and contact preferences.
  • Drug Mgmt: Copay Assistance Program (CAP) tracking.

Claim Lookup Blade

  • Search by member, RX number, NDC, pharmacy NPI, or date range.
  • Use filters to narrow results (trial vs production, pending, etc.).
  • Best practice: Start broad → then narrow.

Claim Information Blade

  • Response tab: Copay, ingredient cost, dispensing fee, deductible, OOP, PA number.
  • DUR tab: Drug Utilization Review alerts.
  • COB tab: Coordination of Benefits details.

1.3 MHK (Member Health Key)

MHK is used for prior authorizations, appeals, and clinical documentation.

Core Functions

  • Create and manage PA cases.
  • Submit appeals and RFIs.
  • Document clinical decisions.

Common Workflow

  1. Open member profile.
  2. Navigate to Care → Pharmacy Authorization → Add.
  3. Enter NDC, quantity, duration, diagnosis, and provider info.
  4. Select priority (standard or expedited).

1.4 Advocate System

Advocate is the communication and ticketing system for documenting all member interactions.

Key Features

  • Filters: Service flag, subject category, queue, status.
  • Ticket claiming: “Get Next Ticket” or “Claim & Work”.
  • Documentation: Every call, email, override, and PA must be logged.

Section 2: Member Interaction Fundamentals

This module covers the essential skills required to interact with members professionally, accurately, and with empathy. You will learn call flow, HIPAA verification, tone control, and how to guide members through pharmacy-related issues.

2.1 Standard Call Flow

A consistent call flow ensures accuracy, compliance, and a positive member experience.

Call Flow Steps

  1. Greeting: Warm, professional, and branded.
  2. Verification: Complete HIPAA verification before discussing PHI.
  3. Issue Discovery: Ask clarifying questions to understand the member’s need.
  4. Resolution: Research, troubleshoot, and provide clear next steps.
  5. Recap: Summarize what you did and what the member can expect.
  6. Closing: End the call with appreciation and professionalism.

Example Greeting

“Thank you for calling Rightway Pharmacy Support, this is Chandra. How may I assist you today?”

2.2 HIPAA Verification

HIPAA verification must be completed before discussing any protected health information (PHI).

Required Verification Elements

  • Member’s full name
  • Date of birth
  • Address or phone number on file
  • Relationship to cardholder (if dependent)

Verification Script

“Before we continue, I need to verify your account. Can you please confirm your full name, date of birth, and address on file?”

When Verification Fails

  • Do not disclose any PHI.
  • Offer to contact the cardholder.
  • Document the failed verification in Advocate.

2.3 Empathy, Tone, and De‑Escalation

Members may be frustrated, confused, or dealing with urgent medication needs. Your tone sets the tone for the entire interaction.

Empathy Phrases

  • “I understand how important this medication is for you.”
  • “Thank you for your patience while I look into this.”
  • “I can imagine how frustrating that must feel.”

De‑Escalation Techniques

  • Lower your voice to calm the member.
  • Acknowledge their concern before offering solutions.
  • Use “we” language to build partnership.

2.4 Language Services

Rightway provides interpreter services for members who prefer a language other than English.

Workflow

  1. Identify the member’s preferred language.
  2. Initiate interpreter services through the approved vendor.
  3. Remain on the line to guide the conversation.
  4. Document interpreter usage in Advocate.

2.5 Documentation Standards

Every interaction must be documented clearly and accurately in Advocate.

Required Elements

  • Reason for the call
  • Actions taken
  • Systems used (Tredium, MHK, etc.)
  • Outcome and next steps
  • Any escalations or transfers

Section 3: Eligibility & Benefits Research

This module teaches you how to verify member eligibility, interpret benefit structures, understand cost‑share components, and navigate accumulators. These skills are essential for resolving pharmacy‑related questions accurately.

3.1 Eligibility Basics

Eligibility determines whether a member is active and covered under their pharmacy plan.

Where to Check Eligibility

  • Tredium → Patient Blade: Shows active/inactive status.
  • Effective Dates: Coverage start and end dates.
  • Plan Type: Commercial, Medicare, Medicaid, Employer‑sponsored.

Common Eligibility Issues

  • Coverage terminated
  • Future effective date
  • Incorrect pharmacy benefit (medical vs pharmacy)
  • Dependent not covered

3.2 Plan Types & What They Mean

Commercial Plans

  • Employer‑sponsored or individual plans
  • May include deductibles, copays, or coinsurance
  • Formularies vary by employer group

Medicare Part D

  • Includes deductible, initial coverage, coverage gap, and catastrophic phases
  • Strict formulary rules
  • Often requires prior authorization

Medicaid

  • State‑specific rules
  • Preferred drug lists (PDLs)
  • Strict quantity limits

3.3 Accumulators (Deductible & Out‑of‑Pocket)

Accumulators track how much a member has paid toward their plan’s cost‑share requirements.

Key Terms

  • Deductible: Amount the member must pay before insurance contributes.
  • Out‑of‑Pocket Maximum (OOP): Once reached, the plan pays 100%.
  • Coinsurance: Percentage the member pays after deductible.
  • Copay: Fixed amount per prescription.

Where to Find Accumulators

  • Tredium → Benefit Balances
  • Shows deductible remaining, OOP remaining, and amounts applied

Example

Scenario: Member has a $500 deductible. They have paid $300 so far.

Deductible remaining: $200

3.4 Understanding Cost Share

Cost share is the amount the member pays for a medication based on their plan design.

Cost Share Components

  • Copay
  • Coinsurance
  • Deductible
  • Tier level (generic, preferred brand, non‑preferred brand, specialty)

Where to Find Cost Share

  • Tredium → Claim Information → Response Tab
  • Shows copay, ingredient cost, dispensing fee, deductible applied, and OOP applied

3.5 Formulary & Tiering

The formulary determines which medications are covered and at what tier.

Common Tier Structure

  • Tier 1: Generic
  • Tier 2: Preferred brand
  • Tier 3: Non‑preferred brand
  • Tier 4: Specialty

Where to Check Formulary

  • Client‑specific formulary documents
  • Tredium → Drug Management
  • Plan reference materials

Section 4: Claims Research & Troubleshooting

This module teaches you how to research pharmacy claims, interpret claim responses, identify reject codes, and troubleshoot issues using Tredium and other systems.

4.1 Claim Statuses

Understanding claim statuses helps you determine what happened during processing.

Common Statuses

  • Paid: Claim processed successfully.
  • Rejected: Claim denied; requires troubleshooting.
  • Reversed: Pharmacy canceled the claim.
  • Duplicate: Same claim submitted twice.

Where to Find Status

  • Tredium → Claim Information → Response Tab

4.2 DUR (Drug Utilization Review) Codes

DUR codes alert the pharmacist to potential safety issues.

Common DUR Codes

  • DD: Drug–Drug interaction
  • HD: High dose
  • LD: Low dose
  • PA: Prior authorization required

Where to View DUR

  • Tredium → Claim Information → DUR Tab

4.3 Reject Codes & How to Resolve Them

Reject codes explain why a claim failed. Each code has a specific meaning and resolution path.

Common Rejects

  • 70: Product/Service Not Covered
  • 75: Prior Authorization Required
  • 76: Plan Limitations Exceeded
  • 79: Refill Too Soon
  • 88: DUR Reject

Resolution Examples

Reject 75: Submit PA through MHK or advise provider.

Reject 79: Provide refill date and document in Advocate.

Reject 70: Check formulary or alternative options.

4.4 Coordination of Benefits (COB)

COB determines which insurance pays first when a member has multiple plans.

Where to Check COB

  • Tredium → COB Tab
  • Shows primary vs secondary coverage

Common COB Issues

  • Incorrect primary plan
  • Outdated coordination information
  • Pharmacy billed wrong plan

4.5 Overrides

Overrides allow claims to process when certain conditions are met.

Common Overrides

  • DAW: Dispense as written
  • Emergency Fill: Temporary supply when PA is pending
  • Vacation Override: Early fill for travel

When Overrides Apply

  • Member traveling
  • System outage
  • Provider unavailable
  • PA pending but urgent need

Section 5: Prior Authorizations (PA)

This module explains when prior authorizations are required, how to check PA status, how to submit a PA, and how to communicate PA requirements to members and providers.

5.1 What Is a Prior Authorization?

A prior authorization (PA) is a requirement from the insurance plan that the provider must obtain approval before the medication can be covered.

Common Reasons for PA Requirements

  • High-cost medications
  • Specialty drugs
  • Non-preferred brand medications
  • Quantity limits exceeded
  • Step therapy requirements

5.2 How to Check PA Status

Where to Check

  • MHK → Authorization Search
  • Tredium → Drug Management
  • Client-specific PA portals (if applicable)

PA Status Types

  • Approved: Claim should process.
  • Denied: Member or provider may appeal.
  • Pending: Waiting for clinical review.
  • Not Found: No PA submitted yet.

5.3 How to Submit a PA

Most PAs are submitted through MHK or by fax using client-specific forms.

PA Submission Workflow

  1. Gather required clinical information from the provider.
  2. Submit PA through MHK → New Authorization.
  3. Attach clinical notes or supporting documents.
  4. Confirm submission and provide tracking number.
  5. Document the interaction in Advocate.

Information Needed for PA Submission

  • Diagnosis
  • Medication name and strength
  • Directions for use
  • Previous medications tried (step therapy)
  • Clinical justification

5.4 How to Explain PA Requirements to Members

Member-Friendly Script

“Your plan requires approval from your provider before this medication can be covered. I can help you understand the next steps.”

Key Points to Communicate

  • The provider must submit the PA.
  • The plan reviews the request based on clinical guidelines.
  • Approval is not guaranteed.
  • Processing time varies by plan.

Typical PA Timelines

  • Standard: 3–5 business days
  • Expedited: 24–72 hours
  • Medicare: Follows CMS timelines

5.5 Appeals

If a PA is denied, the member or provider may request an appeal.

Appeal Workflow

  1. Review denial reason in MHK.
  2. Explain appeal rights to the member.
  3. Provide appeal instructions or forms.
  4. Document the interaction in Advocate.

Section 6: Pharmacy Network & Pricing

This module explains how pharmacy networks work, how pricing is calculated, and why members may see different costs at different pharmacies.

6.1 Pharmacy Network Basics

Pharmacy networks determine which pharmacies members can use at the lowest cost.

Network Types

  • Retail: Local pharmacies (CVS, Walgreens, Walmart, etc.)
  • Mail Order: 90‑day supplies shipped to the member
  • Specialty: High‑cost or complex medications

In‑Network vs Out‑of‑Network

  • In‑Network: Lower cost, contracted rates
  • Out‑of‑Network: Higher cost, sometimes not covered

Where to Check Network Status

  • Tredium → Pharmacy Lookup
  • Shows network tier and contract status

6.2 Usual & Customary (U&C) Pricing

U&C is the pharmacy’s cash price — the amount a member would pay without insurance.

Key Points

  • Insurance will not pay more than the U&C price.
  • If U&C is lower than the copay, the member pays the U&C.
  • U&C varies by pharmacy.

Example

Copay: $15

U&C: $9

Member Pays: $9 (because U&C is lower)

6.3 Maximum Allowable Cost (MAC) Pricing

MAC pricing is used for generics to control costs.

Key Points

  • MAC sets the maximum amount the plan will pay for a generic drug.
  • If the pharmacy’s cost is higher than MAC, the member may pay more.
  • MAC varies by plan and drug.

Example

MAC Price: $12

Pharmacy Cost: $18

Member Pays: $6 difference + copay

6.4 DAW (Dispense As Written) Codes

DAW codes affect pricing and determine whether a generic substitution is allowed.

Common DAW Codes

  • DAW 0: No product selection indicated
  • DAW 1: Provider requests brand
  • DAW 2: Member requests brand

Impact on Pricing

  • DAW 1 often requires brand copay or PA
  • DAW 2 may increase cost significantly
  • DAW 0 usually allows generic substitution

6.5 Why Prices Differ Between Pharmacies

Factors That Affect Pricing

  • Pharmacy contract rates
  • U&C differences
  • MAC pricing variations
  • DAW codes
  • Pharmacy fees

How to Explain This to Members

“Pharmacies set their own cash prices, and your plan pays based on contracted rates. That’s why costs can vary from one pharmacy to another.”

Section 7: Medication Safety & Quality

This module focuses on medication safety, DUR alerts, high‑risk medications, and best practices for preventing errors and supporting members with accurate, safety‑focused information.

7.1 DUR Alerts

DUR (Drug Utilization Review) alerts help identify potential safety issues before a medication is dispensed.

Common DUR Alerts

  • DD: Drug–Drug interaction
  • HD: High dose
  • LD: Low dose
  • PA: Prior authorization required
  • ER: Early refill concern

Where to View DUR Alerts

  • Tredium → Claim Information → DUR Tab

7.2 High‑Risk Medications

Some medications require extra caution due to their potency, side effects, or potential for misuse.

Examples of High‑Risk Medications

  • Opioids
  • Anticoagulants (blood thinners)
  • Insulin
  • Immunosuppressants
  • Specialty medications

Safety Considerations

  • Verify correct dosage and directions
  • Check for interactions
  • Ensure PA or step therapy requirements are met
  • Document all safety concerns in Advocate

7.3 Error Prevention

Preventing medication errors is a shared responsibility between the pharmacy, provider, and support team.

Common Error Types

  • Wrong medication
  • Incorrect dosage
  • Duplicate therapy
  • Allergy conflicts
  • Incorrect quantity

How PCSRs Help Prevent Errors

  • Review DUR alerts carefully
  • Confirm medication details with members
  • Escalate safety concerns to pharmacists
  • Document accurately in Advocate

7.4 Counseling Points

While PCSRs do not provide clinical advice, they can reinforce key safety messages.

General Counseling Reinforcement

  • Take medication exactly as prescribed
  • Do not stop medication without provider guidance
  • Report side effects to provider
  • Store medication safely

Member-Friendly Script

“For your safety, please follow the directions on your prescription label and contact your provider if you notice anything unusual.”

7.5 Quality Standards

Quality standards ensure members receive safe, effective, and timely care.

Key Standards

  • Accurate documentation
  • Timely follow‑up
  • Clear communication
  • Escalation when needed

Section 8: Specialty Medications & Support

Specialty medications require special handling, additional approvals, and high‑touch support. This module teaches you how to assist members who rely on these complex therapies.

8.1 What Are Specialty Medications?

Specialty medications are high‑cost, high‑complexity drugs used to treat chronic or rare conditions.

Common Specialty Categories

  • Oncology
  • Rheumatology
  • Multiple sclerosis
  • HIV
  • Hepatitis C
  • Immunology

Key Characteristics

  • High cost
  • Strict storage requirements
  • Frequent monitoring
  • Often require PA or step therapy

8.2 Specialty Pharmacy Workflow

Specialty pharmacies provide high‑touch support for complex medications.

Workflow Steps

  1. Prescription received by specialty pharmacy
  2. Benefits investigation (BI)
  3. Prior authorization review
  4. Copay assistance screening
  5. Clinical onboarding call
  6. Shipment scheduling

Where to Check Specialty Status

  • Tredium → Specialty Notes
  • MHK → Authorization Details

8.3 Cold‑Chain Handling

Many specialty medications require refrigeration or temperature‑controlled shipping.

Key Points

  • Must remain within a specific temperature range
  • Shipped with ice packs or temperature monitors
  • Missed deliveries may require replacement
  • Members must be home to receive shipment

Member-Friendly Script

“This medication requires temperature‑controlled shipping, so it’s important that someone is available to receive the package.”

8.4 Copay Assistance Programs

Specialty medications often have high out‑of‑pocket costs. Many manufacturers offer financial assistance.

Types of Assistance

  • Manufacturer copay cards
  • Patient assistance programs (PAP)
  • Foundation grants

How PCSRs Support Members

  • Provide program contact information
  • Explain eligibility requirements
  • Document assistance in Advocate

8.5 Specialty PA Requirements

Specialty medications almost always require prior authorization.

Common Requirements

  • Diagnosis confirmation
  • Lab results
  • Previous therapies tried
  • Clinical justification

Where to Check PA Status

  • MHK → Authorization Search
  • Tredium → Drug Management

Section 9: Pharmacy Operations & Member Support

This module covers the operational workflows that PCSRs handle daily, including refills, transfers, overrides, and communication with pharmacies and members.

9.1 Refill Requests

Members often call to request refills or ask why a refill is not available.

Common Refill Issues

  • No refills remaining
  • Refill too soon
  • Quantity limits
  • Provider discontinued medication

How to Support Members

  • Check claim history in Tredium
  • Verify refill dates and quantity
  • Explain refill-too-soon logic
  • Advise member to contact provider if no refills remain

9.2 Prescription Transfers

Members may request to move prescriptions between pharmacies.

Transfer Workflow

  1. Member chooses new pharmacy
  2. New pharmacy contacts old pharmacy
  3. Prescription is transferred electronically or by phone
  4. Claim is reprocessed at new pharmacy

Key Points

  • PCSRs cannot transfer prescriptions directly
  • Controlled substances may have restrictions
  • Some states limit transfer frequency

9.3 Overrides

Overrides allow claims to process when certain conditions are met.

Common Overrides

  • Vacation Override: Early fill for travel
  • Emergency Fill: Temporary supply when PA is pending
  • Lost Medication Override: Case-by-case approval

How to Support Members

  • Check plan rules in Tredium
  • Explain override eligibility
  • Document override requests in Advocate

9.4 Communicating with Pharmacies

PCSRs often need to contact pharmacies to clarify claim issues or verify information.

Reasons to Contact a Pharmacy

  • Verify claim submission
  • Confirm NDC used
  • Check stock availability
  • Clarify DAW codes

Professional Script

“Hi, this is Chandra calling from Rightway Pharmacy Support. I’m calling to verify a claim for one of our members.”

9.5 Member Support Best Practices

Clear communication and accurate information help members feel supported and confident.

Best Practices

  • Use empathy and active listening
  • Provide clear next steps
  • Avoid jargon
  • Document all interactions in Advocate

Section 10: Advanced Scenarios & Call Handling

This module prepares PCSRs to handle complex, multi‑layered situations using critical thinking, system navigation, and confident communication.

10.1 Multi‑System Research

Advanced calls often require checking multiple systems to piece together the full story.

Common System Combinations

  • Tredium + MHK: Claim + PA status
  • Tredium + Advocate: Claim + documentation history
  • MHK + Provider Call: PA + clinical clarification

Best Practices

  • Start with Tredium to understand the claim
  • Check MHK if PA or step therapy is involved
  • Review Advocate for previous notes
  • Document your findings clearly

10.2 Complex Member Situations

Some calls require deeper investigation and careful communication.

Examples

  • Conflicting PA information
  • Multiple pharmacies billing different NDCs
  • High‑cost specialty medication delays
  • COB issues causing unexpected denials

How to Approach These Calls

  • Stay calm and confident
  • Gather all details before explaining anything
  • Use system notes to reconstruct the timeline
  • Escalate when safety or compliance is involved

10.3 Escalation Logic

Knowing when to escalate is essential for safety, accuracy, and member trust.

Escalate When:

  • There is a potential safety issue
  • A DUR alert requires pharmacist review
  • A provider or pharmacy requests clinical input
  • A member is in crisis or medically urgent situation
  • System errors prevent claim processing

Escalation Script

“To make sure you receive the safest and most accurate support, I’m going to escalate this to our clinical team.”

10.4 Scenario Practice

Scenario 1: Conflicting PA Information

Situation: Member says PA was approved, but claim still rejects.

Steps:

  • Check MHK for approval dates
  • Verify NDC used by pharmacy
  • Confirm PA matches drug strength and quantity
  • Advise pharmacy to resubmit claim

Scenario 2: Refill Too Soon + Lost Medication

Steps:

  • Check last fill date in Tredium
  • Confirm plan rules for lost medication overrides
  • Explain next steps to member
  • Document in Advocate

Scenario 3: High‑Cost Specialty Delay

Steps:

  • Check PA status
  • Check specialty pharmacy notes
  • Verify shipping schedule
  • Provide clear timeline to member
